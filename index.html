Water Pollution

Water pollution is a pressing environmental issue that continues to plague our planet. It occurs when harmful substances enter water bodies and degrade their quality, rendering them unfit for human consumption and detrimental to aquatic ecosystems. Industrial waste, sewage, agricultural runoff, and improper disposal of chemicals are major contributors to water pollution. The consequences of water pollution are far-reaching. Contaminated water poses a severe threat to public health, leading to waterborne diseases and illnesses. Aquatic life suffers immensely as pollutants disrupt ecosystems, leading to the decline of fish populations and the destruction of fragile coral reefs. Additionally, polluted water affects agriculture, as it is used for irrigation and livestock consumption, ultimately compromising food safety. In conclusion, water pollution poses a significant threat to our planet's well-being. It is imperative that we take immediate action to mitigate its effects through regulation, education, and sustainable practices to safeguard our water resources for future generations.

Separation of Wastage

Proper separation of waste is a vital step in managing our environmental footprint. By segregating waste into different categories, such as recyclables, organic waste, and non-recyclables, we can significantly reduce the negative impact on our planet. Recycling allows us to conserve resources, reduce energy consumption, and minimize landfill waste. Organic waste can be composted, enriching the soil and reducing the need for chemical fertilizers. Non-recyclables should be disposed of properly, following waste management guidelines. Implementing efficient waste separation practices requires education and awareness among individuals, as well as the support of local governments and communities. By embracing proper waste separation, we can contribute to a healthier and more sustainable future.

Swachh Bharat Abhiyan

Swachh Bharat Abhiyan, or the Clean India Mission, is a nationwide campaign initiated by the Government of India to address the pressing issue of cleanliness and sanitation. Launched in 2014, the campaign aims to make India clean and free from open defecation, litter, and unhygienic practices. It focuses on promoting proper waste management, constructing toilets, and creating awareness about hygiene and cleanliness. Swachh Bharat Abhiyan has had a significant impact, improving sanitation facilities and creating a cleaner environment across the country. However, the success of this mission relies on the active participation of individuals, communities, and local authorities. By working together, we can continue to make strides towards a cleaner and healthier India.

ABSTRACT

Visited Swachh Bharat Mission (SBM), Swachh Bharat Abhiyan, or Clean India Mission is a country-wide campaign initiated by the Government of India in 2014 to eliminate open defecation and improve solid waste management. The program also aims to increase awareness of menstrual health management. It is a restructured version of the Nirmal Bharat Abhiyan launched in 2009 that failed to achieve its intended targets. Phase 1 of the Swachh Bharat Mission lasted till October 2019. Phase 2 is being implemented between 2020-21 and 2024-25 to help cement the work of Phase 1. Initiated by the Government of India, the mission aimed to achieve an "open-defecation free" (ODF) India by 2 October 2019, the 150th anniversary of the birth of Mahatma Gandhi through construction of toilets. An estimated 89.9 million toilets were built in the period. The objectives of the first phase of the mission also included eradication of manual scavenging, generating awareness and bringing about a behavior change regarding sanitation practices, and augmentation of capacity at the local level. The second phase of the mission aims to sustain the open defecation free status and improve the management of solid and liquid waste, while also working to improve the lives of sanitation workers. The mission is aimed at progressing towards target 6.2 of the Sustainable Development Goals Number 6 established by the United Nations in 2015. By achieving the lowest open defecation-free status in 2019, India achieved its Sustainable Development Goal (SDG) 6.2 health target in record time, eleven years ahead of the UN SDG target of 31 December 2030.
